Computing, especially scientific computing, tends to be associated with neutrality and objectivity. There is a sense that they are detached from ‘flawed’ human senses and reasoning. Johanna Drucker argues that this is especially obvious (and dangerous) in the domain of data visualisation. The data are presented free of context -- the context in which it was created and the context of its creator.

Drucker argues that “[b]ecause realist approaches to visualization assume transparency and equivalence, as if the phenomenal world were self-evident and the apprehension of it a mere mechanical task, they are fundamentally at odds with approaches to humanities scholarship premised on constructivist principles”. As a counter, she calls for the new paradigms of visualisation which forefront positionality and context and offers several prototypic examples.

A ‘standard’ bar chart, for Drucker, is lacking, in that it only displays a single discrete measurement without giving any sense of how that value was arrived at -- of the ‘constructedness’ of the data. Her alternative version, depicting the ‘appearance’ of 19th century novels augments a ‘typical’ chart whose vertical bars represent the number of novels whole publication date fell within a given year with sometimes disjoint horizontal bars made up of distinct symbols each representing a step of the publication process (writing, editing, pre-press etc.); This addition is intended to contextualise the vertical bar, elucidating its composition.

Another example mutates standard units of measurement to introduce a new type of axis that, rather than being constrained to linear and uniform progression, twists and warps to reflect human perception of time.

“So naturalized are the Google maps and bar charts generated from spread sheets that they pass as unquestioned representations of what ‘is’”, argues Drucker; this, she asserts, is problematic in DH because these ‘standard’ representations were developed in a non-humanistic epistemological setting and correspondingly embody values and assumptions that often oppose those of humanistic inquiry -- namely, that information can be decontextual and observer independent. In DH, it is important to critically attend to how contexts and perspectives are implicitly or explicitly represented.
